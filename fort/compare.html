How Fort compares

The auth market splits into polished SaaS vendors that hold your users, and heavyweight open source that holds your weekends. Fort takes the third path: a self-hosted, Supabase-compatible auth authority that serves every app you run — with no per-user meter.

Strong tools, different jobs

Each of these platforms is genuinely strong on its home turf. Auth0 is the benchmark for managed enterprise CIAM, with best-in-class docs and federation. Clerk has the finest prebuilt React auth components in the market. Keycloak covers more identity protocols than anyone — full SAML, brokering, LDAP. Supabase Auth is unbeatable value inside a Supabase project.

What none of them offers is a self-hosted, Supabase-compatible auth authority that serves many apps from one deployment — issuing tokens in one place, verifying them everywhere over a single seam, on your own infrastructure, at a flat cost. That is the gap Fort was built to fill.

Head to head

When each platform is the right call — and when Fort is.

Fort vs Auth0

  • Choose Auth0 if you want fully managed enterprise CIAM with deep SAML/OIDC federation and are comfortable with vendor-cloud residency and enterprise pricing.
  • Choose Fort when the pricing cliffs stop making sense — Auth0's per-MAU tiers climb from thousands into six figures, and cost is the most commonly cited reason teams migrate off it.
  • Auth0 gates MFA and RBAC behind paid tiers and charges $100/mo per extra enterprise SSO connection; Fort ships TOTP, OTP, magic links, and RBAC at every scale.
  • Auth0's "Private Deployment" is Enterprise-only and still Okta-operated; Fort is a single binary you run on-prem or air-gapped, full stop.

Fort vs Clerk

  • Choose Clerk for a greenfield React or Next.js app where its prebuilt components — genuinely the best in the market — get you to a polished login fastest.
  • Choose Fort when auth must serve many heterogeneous apps, not one frontend — one authority with per-platform branding, settings, and isolation.
  • Clerk is cloud-only: no self-hosting, no on-prem, no air-gap, and your user data lives on Clerk's infrastructure — a non-starter for data-residency and sovereignty requirements Fort was built for.
  • Clerk meters at $0.02 per monthly user with B2B organizations as a $100/mo add-on; leaving means exporting users and rebuilding every flow. Fort is flat-cost, and your users are already in your database.

Fort vs Keycloak

  • Choose Keycloak when you need the full protocol surface — SAML 2.0 federation, identity brokering, LDAP/AD — and you staff a platform team to run it. On federation, Keycloak wins outright.
  • Choose Fort for the same self-hosted ownership without the JVM cluster: one Go service plus Postgres instead of hard HA setup and major upgrades that break custom themes.
  • Keycloak's software is free but published TCO estimates run around $200K over three years in operations time; Fort is built to be operationally boring.
  • Keycloak configures dated end-user flows through an admin console; Fort ships modern login, OTP, TOTP, magic-link, PIN, and lock-screen flows — Supabase-compatible out of the box.

Fort vs Supabase Auth

  • Choose Supabase Auth when you're building one product inside a Supabase project — the RLS integration and free tier are unbeatable value there.
  • Choose Fort when auth outgrows the project: Supabase Auth is scoped to a single project with no first-class multi-app or organizations model; Fort serves many platforms from one deployment.
  • Fort issues the same JWT claims and verifies GoTrue tokens, so existing Supabase apps and RLS policies keep working — migration means changing the issuer and secret, not rewriting consumers.
  • Self-hosting Supabase Auth means running the whole Supabase docker stack; Fort is one binary with its own database, plus the enterprise pieces Supabase Auth lacks — full RBAC, audit log, and admin surface.
